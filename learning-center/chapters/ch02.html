How to use this: This chapter is mostly memorization of names, numbers, and "which document does what." There is very little hands-on skill here - it is the legal/paperwork backbone of being a mechanic. I define every term the first time it shows up, give a plain analogy, then box the must-memorize items in red. Hit the red boxes hard; those are where exam questions live.

1. The Big Picture: Where the Rules Live (Title 14 CFR)

CFR = Code of Federal Regulations. Think of it as the giant rulebook for the whole U.S. government, split into 50 numbered "Titles." Each Title is one subject area.

Title 14 is the one we care about: "Aeronautics and Space." That is where all the FAA's aircraft rules live. (p. 2-1)

Analogy: Title 14 is like the "Aviation" shelf in a giant library. Within that shelf are individual books called Parts (Part 21, Part 43, Part 91, etc.). Within each book are sections (like 43.9, 91.417).

How Title 14 is laid out

  • Title 14 has five volumes. The first three volumes (containing 75 active regulations / Parts) are the FAA. Volume 4 = Office of the Secretary of DOT + Commercial Space. Volume 5 = NASA. (p. 2-1)
  • The FAA's airworthiness rules sort into three categories: (1) Administrative, (2) Airworthiness Certification, (3) Airworthiness Operation. (p. 2-1)
  • Old name: "FARs" = Federal Aviation Regulations. The FAA now says "14 CFR part XX" instead, because Title 48 ("Federal Acquisitions Regulations") also abbreviates to "FAR" and caused confusion. (p. 2-1)
  • Most Part numbers and sections are ODD-numbered on purpose - so the FAA can slot in new even-numbered rules later (you'll see this with 43.10, 43.12, 135.422 - "note the even number" = added later). (pp. 2-1, 2-16)
  • SFAR = Special Federal Aviation Regulation: a narrow rule for one unique situation, usually with a time limit. The SFAR number is just a sequential count - it has no link to the rule it attaches to. (p. 2-1)

MUST KNOW - Section 1

  • 14 CFR = Title 14 of the Code of Federal Regulations = where ALL FAA rules live ("Aeronautics & Space").
  • Three categories of regs: Administrative, Airworthiness Certification, Airworthiness Operation.
  • The 3 BIG regulations that govern airworthiness: Part 21 (certification of products/parts), Part 43 (maintenance), Part 91 (operating rules). Memorize these three together. (p. 2-1, 2-2)
  • SFAR = Special FAR = narrow, usually time-limited rule.

2. Part 3 - General Requirements (What "Airworthy" Means)

Part 3 is short but huge: it finally defined the word "airworthy" in a regulation. Before this, the term was only implied (e.g., in Part 21 section 21.183 and on the airworthiness certificate). (p. 2-1)

Airworthy = the aircraft (1) conforms to its type design AND (2) is in a condition for safe operation. Both halves are required. (14 CFR 3.5; p. 2-1)

Two more Part 3 definitions:

  • Product = an aircraft, aircraft engine, or aircraft propeller. (Just those three.)
  • Record = basically any way information is preserved - paper, microfilm, dataplate, stamped mark, bar code, or electronic format.

Two kinds of airworthiness (these two phrases run through the whole chapter):

  • Original airworthiness = getting the design/aircraft approved and certified the first time (Part 21 land).
  • Recurrent airworthiness = keeping it airworthy day-to-day over its life (Part 43 + Part 91 land). (pp. 2-1, 2-3)

MUST KNOW - Section 2

  • AIRWORTHY = conforms to type design + in condition for safe operation. Both parts. This is the single most-tested definition in the chapter. (Part 3.5, p. 2-1)
  • Product = aircraft, engine, OR propeller (only those three).
  • Original airworthiness (Part 21) vs. recurrent airworthiness (Parts 43 & 91).

3. Regulation vs. Guidance (the most important distinction in the chapter)

Analogy: A regulation is the LAW - the speed limit sign. Guidance is the driver's-ed manual that tells you a good way to obey the law. You MUST obey the law. You don't have to follow the manual word-for-word, but following it keeps you legal and safe.

REGULATION (the law)GUIDANCE (advice)
Examples14 CFR Parts (21, 43, 91...), Airworthiness Directives (AD) Advisory Circulars (AC), SAIBs, FAA handbooks, manufacturer Service Bulletins
Binding?Mandatory - you can be violated for not following it Not mandatory - "acceptable" methods, NOT the only methods... unless it's referenced into a reg
Key wording"No person may...", "shall", "must" "acceptable to the Administrator", "recommends", "guidance"

The trap: Guidance becomes mandatory if it is "incorporated by reference" into a regulation. Example: an AD is mandatory (it's a reg, Part 39) even though it's printed in the Federal Register, not in the rulebook itself. (pp. 2-24, 2-25, 2-26)

MUST KNOW - Section 3

  • Regulations = mandatory (the law). Guidance = advisory (not mandatory) UNLESS referenced into a reg.
  • AC = advisory (not binding). AD = mandatory (it IS a regulation - Part 39).
  • The phrase "acceptable to the Administrator" signals guidance/acceptable data, NOT a hard requirement.

4. The Three Big Regulations - Up Close

Part 21 - Certification Procedures for Products and Articles

This is the "birth certificate" Part - how a design gets approved. It covers Type Certificates (TC), Supplemental Type Certificates (STC), production certificates, airworthiness certificates, and import/export approvals. Also PMA (Parts Manufacturer Approval) and TSO (Technical Standard Order) parts. Its biggest job is original airworthiness. (pp. 2-3, 2-4)

  • Section 21.50 = "Instructions for Continued Airworthiness." Any STC made after 1981 must come with a full set of ICA (Instructions for Continued Airworthiness) - the maintenance/inspection instructions for the altered part. ICA is built from a 16-subject checklist. (pp. 2-3, 2-9)

Part 43 - Maintenance, Preventive Maintenance, Rebuilding, and Alteration

This is the heart of your job. It is the standard for maintaining every civil aircraft registered in the U.S. It has 13 rules and 6 appendices. (p. 2-8) (Full detail in Section 7 below.)

Part 91 - General Operating and Flight Rules

This is an operating Part aimed at the owner/operator/pilot, but the mechanic must know it because Subpart E (sections 91.401-91.421) is all about maintenance, preventive maintenance, and alterations. Section 91.403(b) ties Part 91 directly to Part 43: no one may do maintenance except as Part 43 requires. 91.7(a): "No person may operate a civil aircraft unless it is in an airworthy condition." (pp. 2-10, 2-21)

Quick map of the other Parts you'll see named (don't memorize all - just recognize them): Part 1 = Definitions; Part 23 = small airplanes (≤12,500 lb, or commuter ≤19,000 lb / ≤19 seats); Part 25 = large/transport airplanes (>12,500 lb); Part 27 = small rotorcraft (≤7,000 lb); Part 29 = large rotorcraft (>7,000 lb); Part 33 = engines; Part 35 = propellers; Part 39 = ADs; Part 45 = ID/registration marking; Part 47 = aircraft registration; Part 65 = mechanics & other airmen; Part 121/125/135 = air carriers/operators; Part 145 = repair stations; Part 147 = AMT schools; Part 183 = FAA designees. (pp. 2-3 to 2-19)

MUST KNOW - Section 4

  • Part 21 = certification (TC, STC, PMA, TSO) - mostly ORIGINAL airworthiness. 21.50 requires ICA.
  • Part 43 = maintenance - "the heart of aviation maintenance"; 13 rules + 6 appendices.
  • Part 91 = operating rules; Subpart E (91.401-91.421) is the maintenance subpart; 91.403(b) forces compliance with Part 43; 91.7(a) = no operating unless airworthy.
  • ICA = Instructions for Continued Airworthiness (16-subject checklist); required on STCs made after 1981.
  • Weight cutoffs: Part 23 small airplane = 12,500 lb or less; Part 25 large = over 12,500 lb; Part 27 rotorcraft = 7,000 lb or less; Part 29 = over 7,000 lb.

5. Civil Air Regulations (CAR) - the Old Rulebook

Before today's 14 CFR existed, aircraft were certified under the CARs (Civil Air Regulations), the rules of the 1940s-60s. Old aircraft are still legally certified under the CAR they were built to, so a mechanic may still need a CAR as the reference for older airplanes. (pp. 2-23, 2-24)

  • CAR 3 - "Airplane Airworthiness - Normal, Utility, Aerobatic, and Restricted Purpose Categories." It is the ancestor of today's Part 23. Examples of CAR 3 aircraft: Piper PA-22/28/32; Cessna 182, 195, 310. (p. 2-23)
  • CAR 4a - "Airplane Airworthiness" (originated 1936, last amended Dec 15, 1952). It and CAR 4b became the regs for large aircraft. So: CAR 3 = small aircraft, CAR 4a/4b = large aircraft. (p. 2-23)
Watch the trap: "CAR" has two meanings - Civil Air Regulations (old U.S.) AND Canadian Aviation Regulations. Know which one a question means. (p. 2-23) Also: a CAM (Civil Aeronautics Manual) contained BOTH regulation and advisory material in one document. (p. 2-2)

MUST KNOW - Section 5

  • CAR 3 -> Part 23 (small aircraft). CAR 4a / 4b -> large aircraft (CAR 4b is the ancestor of Part 25).
  • Old aircraft are still certified to the CAR they were built under - it can still be the maintenance reference.
  • "CAR" is a double meaning: Civil Air Regulations OR Canadian Aviation Regulations.

6. Suspected Unapproved Parts (SUP)

There are four kinds of parts (memorize this list - it's a classic): (p. 2-24)

  1. Good part, good paperwork -> approved, install it.
  2. Good part, BAD paperwork -> SUP (suspected unapproved).
  3. BAD part, "good" (bogus) paperwork -> SUP (suspected unapproved).
  4. Bad part, bad paperwork -> unapproved - NEVER install.

The middle two are the Suspected Unapproved Parts (SUP) - if EITHER the part OR its paperwork is questionable, it's a SUP.

What to do with a SUP: tell the shop foreman / supervisor / quality person; segregate and quarantine the part until proper disposition is decided; contacting the manufacturer helps gather facts. Reference: AC 21-29, "Detecting and Reporting Suspected Unapproved Parts." Report a SUP at www.faa.gov. (p. 2-24)

MUST KNOW - Section 6

  • 4 part types; the middle two (good part/bad paperwork & bad part/bogus paperwork) = SUPs.
  • SUP action: SEGREGATE and QUARANTINE, notify supervisor/quality, then determine disposition.
  • Reference doc = AC 21-29.

7. Inside Part 43 - The Maintenance Rules That Run Your Career

Here is what each Part 43 section actually does, in plain English. (pp. 2-14 to 2-20)

SectionWhat it does (plain English)
43.1Applicability. Part 43 does NOT apply to an aircraft that has only ever held an experimental certificate (pink Form 8130-7). But if a Standard-cert aircraft is temporarily put into experimental, Part 43 still applies. (p. 2-14)
43.2Defines "overhauled" vs "rebuilt." Both require: disassembled, cleaned, inspected, repaired as necessary, reassembled, tested. Difference = how it's tested: OVERHAUL = tested to manufacturer's service limits (used-part limits); REBUILT = tested to same tolerances as a NEW part. (p. 2-14)
43.3Who MAY PERFORM maintenance - 9 persons (mechanic, repairman, person under supervision, repair station, air carrier, pilot doing preventive maint. on own aircraft, etc.). (pp. 2-14, 2-15)
43.5Approval for Return to Service (RTS) - requires a proper record entry per 43.9 or 43.11; may need Form 337 for a major repair/alteration. (p. 2-15)
43.7Who MAY APPROVE for RTS - 7 persons. Note: a repairman may perform work but may NOT approve RTS. (p. 2-16)
43.9Content/form of maintenance record entries (NOT inspections). The What / When / Who rule. (pp. 2-16, 2-17)
43.10Disposition of life-limited parts (note even number - added 2002). Defines "life-limited part" & "life status"; 7 control methods. (p. 2-16)
43.11Content/form of inspection record entries. Also What/When/Who. If item FAILS, a list of discrepancies must be given to the owner. (p. 2-17)
43.12Falsification of records = grounds for suspension/revocation of certificate. "My signature lasts a lifetime." (p. 2-17)
43.13Performance Rules - use the methods in the manufacturer's manual / ICA, or other methods acceptable to the Administrator. Tie-in to AC 43.13-1 / -2. (pp. 2-17, 2-18)
43.15Extra inspection rules - rotorcraft items, annual/100-hour (checklist required), progressive. Defines "routine" (visual, no disassembly) vs "detailed" (thorough, with disassembly). (p. 2-18)
43.16Airworthiness Limitations - must follow the Airworthiness Limitations section of the manual/ICA. (p. 2-18)
43.17Maintenance on U.S. products by certain Canadian persons (AME/AMO) under the U.S.-Canada agreement. (p. 2-18)

Repair vs. Alteration, and Major vs. Minor (the decision that drives your paperwork)

Before any job, ask three questions: (1) What am I going to do? (2) How does the FAA classify it? (3) What documentation does it need? (p. 2-15)

  • Repair = returns the aircraft to its previous/unaltered condition (new tires, brakes, magnetos, even a whole replacement engine that's the correct one). (p. 2-15)
  • Alteration = changes/modifies the aircraft from its previous state (winglets, new avionics, an engine NOT listed on the TCDS). (p. 2-15)
  • Major = could appreciably affect weight, balance, structural strength, performance, powerplant operation, flight characteristics, or other airworthiness qualities (or not done by elementary operations). Minor = everything else. (p. 2-15; Part 43 Appendix A)
Job typeData requiredPaperwork
Minor repair/alterationACCEPTABLE data (manufacturer manual, AC 43.13-1) Ordinary logbook entry (43.9)
Major repair/alterationAPPROVED data (AD, STC, TCDS, DER approval, FAA-approved SB) FAA Form 337

Field Approval: if there's no existing approved data, the mechanic fills the front of Form 337 through block 6 (leaving block 3 open), describes the work in block 8, and sends it to the local FSDO (Flight Standards District Office) for an ASI (Aviation Safety Inspector) to approve in block 3. (pp. 2-15, 2-16)

Preventive maintenance - what it IS: Per Part 43, Appendix A paragraph (c), preventive maintenance is simple/minor preservation operations and replacement of small standard parts NOT involving complex assembly operations. A holder of at least a private pilot certificate may do preventive maintenance on an aircraft they own or operate, as long as it is NOT operated under Part 121, 129, or 135. (A sport pilot may do preventive maintenance only on a light-sport aircraft they own/operate.) The pilot must still make a record entry. (pp. 2-14, 2-16, 2-20)

Part 43 Appendices (recognize what each is)

  • Appendix A - lists Major Alterations, Major Repairs, and Preventive Maintenance. (p. 2-19)
  • Appendix B - Recording of Major Repairs/Alterations (the Form 337 rules). (pp. 2-19, 2-20)
  • Appendix C - Reserved (empty). (p. 2-20)
  • Appendix D - Scope & Detail of Annual and 100-hour inspections (the list is the SAME for both; only who-can-sign differs). 8 detailed areas. (p. 2-20)
  • Appendix E - Altimeter system test ("the 411 test") - every 24 calendar months for IFR. (p. 2-20)
  • Appendix F - ATC transponder test ("the 413 test") - every 24 calendar months. (p. 2-20)

MUST KNOW - Section 7 (Part 43)

  • 43.3 = who may PERFORM (9 persons). 43.7 = who may APPROVE for RTS (7 persons). A repairman may PERFORM but NOT approve RTS.
  • 43.9 = maintenance entries; 43.11 = inspection entries. Both = What (work done) / When (date) / Who (signature, certificate number, type of certificate).
  • OVERHAUL = tested to service limits; REBUILT = tested to NEW-part tolerances.
  • Minor = acceptable data + logbook entry. Major = approved data + FAA Form 337.
  • Repair returns to previous condition; Alteration changes it. Major = appreciably affects weight/balance/strength/performance/etc.
  • 411 test (Appendix E) = altimeter, every 24 months. 413 test (Appendix F) = transponder, every 24 months. Appendix C = reserved. Annual and 100-hour use the SAME Appendix D list.

9. Record-Keeping: TEMPORARY vs PERMANENT (91.417) - the most-tested split

This is the single biggest exam topic in the chapter. 14 CFR 91.417 = how long records are kept. (Compare: 43.9 / 43.11 = how to make the entry; 91.417 = how long to keep it.) (pp. 2-22, 2-23, 2-39, 2-40)

TEMPORARY records
91.417(a)(1) & (b)(1)		PERMANENT records
91.417(a)(2) & (b)(2)
What they are Records of maintenance, preventive maintenance, alterations, and ALL inspections - the routine "what/when/who" entries The big-history records: total time in service (airframe, each engine, each propeller, each rotor); current status of life-limited parts; time since last overhaul of items required to be overhauled; current inspection status; current status of applicable ADs; copies of Form 337 for major alterations
How long kept 1 year from when the work was done - OR until the work is repeated or superseded, whichever comes first Kept as long as you operate the aircraft, and TRANSFERRED WITH THE AIRCRAFT when it is sold
Memory hook "Temporary = the everyday entries that get replaced next time" (1 year / until superseded) "Permanent = the aircraft's life story that travels with it forever"

Two more 91.417 pieces (don't miss these):

  • 91.417(a) (the 411 & 413 testing records): kept 2 years (because those tests are every 24 months). (p. 2-22, Fig. 2-10)
  • 91.417(d): the Form 337 for extended-range fuel tanks installed in the cabin/cargo area must be kept ON BOARD the aircraft at all times. (pp. 2-22, 2-20)
  • 91.417(b)(3): the list of defects/discrepancies given to the owner (per 43.11) is kept until those defects are repaired and the aircraft is approved for RTS. (Fig. 2-10, p. 2-22)
Where records live: Other than the 91.417(d) fuel-tank Form 337, maintenance records do NOT have to be carried on the aircraft - it's actually safer to keep them in an office and back them up (scan/copy) off-site against fire/flood. Records must be made available to the FAA or NTSB on request (91.417(c)). (pp. 2-22, 2-23)

MUST KNOW - Section 9 (the 91.417 split - memorize cold)

  • TEMPORARY = 91.417(a)(1) & (b)(1) = maintenance, preventive maint., alterations, & all inspections = kept 1 YEAR or until the work is repeated/superseded.
  • PERMANENT = 91.417(a)(2) & (b)(2) = total time in service, life-limited parts status, time since overhaul, current inspection status, current AD status, Form 337s = kept while you own it and TRANSFERRED WITH THE AIRCRAFT.
  • 91.417(a) (411/413 test records) = kept 2 years. 91.417(d) = fuel-tank Form 337 stays ON the aircraft.
  • 43.9 / 43.11 = how to make the entry; 91.417 = how long to keep it.
  • Rebuilt engine -> "ZERO TIME" record (91.421).

10. The FAA Document Types (what each one is FOR)

DocumentFull nameWhat it's FOR / key fact
ACAdvisory Circular GUIDANCE, not binding (unless referenced into a reg). Shows an "acceptable" way to comply. Numbered to match the reg subject (e.g., 43.13 relates to Part 43.13). AC system started 1962. (pp. 2-24, 2-25)
ADAirworthiness Directive MANDATORY (it IS a reg - Part 39). Issued for an unsafe condition likely to exist in other products of the same design. Printed in the Federal Register, "incorporated by reference" into Part 39. You may operate the product ONLY after the AD is complied with. (pp. 2-8, 2-26)
SAIBSpecial Airworthiness Information Bulletin Information only - NOT mandatory. Alerts/educates about a concern that does NOT rise to the level of an AD. (p. 2-27)
TCDSType Certificate Data Sheet The aircraft/engine/prop's "spec sheet" - lists limits & conditions for type certification (airspeed, weight, thrust, engine model, fuel type, min crew, etc.). It is PART OF the TC (per 21.41) and is evidence the product was type certificated. First published Jan 1958. (pp. 2-30, 2-31)
STCSupplemental Type Certificate FAA approval of a modification to a type-certificated product. Supplements the original TC; defines the design change, how it affects type design, and serial-number effectivity. The STC & its data are the property of the STC holder - you must get permission to use it. (pp. 2-27, 2-30)
TCType Certificate The FAA's approval of an aircraft/engine/prop design (its "birth certificate"). May only be changed by FAA authorization (that change = an STC). (pp. 2-27, 2-30)
Aircraft Specifications(older recordkeeping document) FAA recordkeeping documents (from the 1926 Air Commerce Act era) for older products. No longer issued but still in effect. Can be converted to a TCDS at the TC holder's option. Subdivided into 5 groups. A specification is NOT part of a TC. (p. 2-27)

How AC and AD numbers are built (recognize the pattern)

  • AC number (e.g., 25-42-C): first part = the FAR subject/Part (25); middle = sequential number within that subject (42nd); last letter = revision (C = 3rd revision; first issue has no letter). (p. 2-26)
  • AD number (3 parts): (1) year of issue, (2) the biweekly period of the year, (3) sequential number within that biweekly period. (p. 2-26)

Three types of AD

  • NPRM (Notice of Proposed Rulemaking) followed by a Final Rule - the standard process (public comment first). (p. 2-26)
  • Final Rule; Request for Comments - urgent enough to adopt immediately (comment period < 60 days), comments taken after. (p. 2-26)
  • Emergency AD - immediate action needed; sent by fax/letter; effective by "actual notice" only to those who receive it; a matching Final Rule AD is published in the Federal Register (usually within 30 days). (p. 2-26)

AMOC = Alternative Method of Compliance: a different technique (or compliance time) than the AD specifies, allowed AFTER FAA approval if it gives an equal level of safety. (p. 2-27)

Non-FAA documents

  • ATA iSpec 2200 (formerly ATA 100) - the airline-industry system that gives standard chapter numbers to aircraft systems so technical data is consistent across large aircraft (e.g., Ch 28 = Fuel, Ch 32 = Landing Gear, Ch 24 = Electrical Power). (pp. 2-30, 2-38)
  • Service Bulletin (SB) - the manufacturer's notice about a defect, service change, or improvement. NOT mandatory unless called out by an AD or required by the inspection program. (Ref 39.27.) (p. 2-33)
  • Structural Repair Manual (SRM) - detailed structural repair limits/criteria; usually a separate manual on large aircraft, often folded into the AMM on small ones. (p. 2-33)
  • Airworthiness Limitations - a separate, clearly titled section of the ICA listing mandatory replacement times, structural inspection intervals, and related procedures. (p. 2-33)
  • Manufacturer manuals: AFM (Airplane Flight Manual - serial-number specific) / POH (Pilot's Operating Handbook - model specific); AMM (Aircraft Maintenance Manual - whole aircraft) / CMM (Component Maintenance Manual - one component, often the overhaul manual). (pp. 2-33, 2-34)

MUST KNOW - Section 10

  • AC = advisory (not mandatory). AD = mandatory (Part 39, unsafe condition likely in same-design products).
  • SAIB = information only, not mandatory (concern below AD level). SB = not mandatory unless an AD or inspection program requires it.
  • TCDS = the spec sheet; it IS part of the TC. STC = approval of a MODIFICATION, supplements the TC. TC = design approval.
  • 3 AD types: NPRM->Final Rule (standard); Final Rule w/ Request for Comments; Emergency AD ("actual notice").
  • AMOC = Alternative Method of Compliance (needs FAA approval).
  • ATA iSpec 2200 = standard chapter numbers for aircraft systems.

11. The FORMS (and the Two Certificates That Live in the Aircraft)

Two kinds: STANDARD = FAA Form 8100-2 (Normal, Utility, Acrobatic, Commuter, Transport, Manned free balloon, Special classes); SPECIAL = FAA Form 8130-7 (Primary, Restricted, Multiple, Limited, Light-sport, Experimental, Special flight permit, Provisional - the experimental cert is PINK). Required whenever a major repair or major alteration is done (the one exception: a Part 145 repair station may use a signed work order + maintenance release instead). Completed at least in duplicate - original to the owner, and a copy goes to the FAA. (Since fall 2005, the technician sends the copy directly to the Aircraft Registration Branch, Oklahoma City; historically a copy went to the local FSDO within 48 hours.) MUST KNOW - Section 11 (forms - memorize the form numbers)

  • Standard Airworthiness Cert = FAA Form 8100-2. Special Airworthiness Cert = FAA Form 8130-7 (experimental = PINK).
  • Registration applied for on Form 8050-1; certificate issued = AC Form 8050-3. Application = 90 days temporary authority.
  • FAA Form 337 = Major Repair and Alteration. Original to owner; copy to FAA (now Oklahoma City Registration Branch directly). Extra fuel-tank copy stays on board.
  • Radio Station License = FCC, required only for flights OUTSIDE the U.S.
  • Airworthiness certificate must be on board AND displayed at the cabin/flight-deck entrance.

12. Records: Making Entries & Electronic Records

  • 43.9 / 43.11 say you MUST make a maintenance/inspection entry; 91.417 says how long to keep it (see Section 9). (p. 2-39)
  • Electronic records are allowed and now common; programs track ADs, SBs, STCs, TCDSs, times in service, AD compliance, etc. The "record" definition in Part 3 already includes electronic format. The technician should know the benefits/limits of these systems. (pp. 2-1, 2-39, 2-40)

13. Light Sport Aircraft (LSA) Maintenance - the Basics

The LSA category (added in 2004) includes gliders, airplanes, gyroplanes, powered parachutes, weight-shift, and lighter-than-air. Two types: SLSA = Special (FACTORY-built) and ELSA = Experimental (KIT-built). (p. 2-40)

How LSA maintenance is different from regular aircraft maintenance (this is the testable contrast):

  • LSA rules come from an industry consensus standard (ASTM), not the usual FAA manuals. The relevant practice is ASTM F2483. (pp. 2-40, 2-41, 2-44)
  • FAA Form 337 is NOT used for LSA major repairs/alterations. Instead the work is evaluated against the ASTM standard and a written affidavit is provided that the LSA still meets the ASTM requirements. (p. 2-45)
  • Safety directives (not ADs) are issued for LSA by the manufacturer, not the FAA. They are mandatory - EXCEPT for experimental-use (ELSA) aircraft. (But if the LSA contains an FAA-TC'd product, the manufacturer must issue a safety directive.) (pp. 2-45, 2-46)
  • Who can work on an LSA (by maintenance level): (pp. 2-44, 2-45)
    • Line maintenance (servicing, simple R&R, the 100-hour/annual condition inspection) - minimum cert = LSA Repairman with INSPECTION rating (may inspect an aircraft they own).
    • Heavy maintenance - a certified mechanic (A and/or P) OR an LSA Repairman with MAINTENANCE rating who has extra task-specific training (this rating can do maintenance AND the 100-hour/annual).
    • Overhaul - only the manufacturer (or someone they authorize); requires a separate overhaul manual.
  • Annual condition inspection = a detailed inspection done once a year per the LSA's maintenance manual. The 100-hour inspection is the same as the annual, just a different interval. (pp. 2-44, 2-45)

MUST KNOW - Section 13 (LSA)

  • SLSA = Special = factory-built. ELSA = Experimental = kit-built. Category added 2004.
  • LSA uses ASTM consensus standards (F2483), NOT Form 337. Major LSA work -> written affidavit of ASTM compliance instead of a 337.
  • LSA gets manufacturer "Safety Directives," not FAA ADs - mandatory except on experimental (ELSA) aircraft.
  • LSA Repairman ratings: INSPECTION (line maint. + 100-hr/annual on aircraft they own) vs MAINTENANCE (maint. + 100-hr/annual, with task-specific training). Overhaul = manufacturer only.

14. The 12 Highest-Yield Facts (your last-minute review)

If you only re-read one box before class, make it this one

  1. Airworthy = conforms to type design + in condition for safe operation. (Part 3.5)
  2. The 3 big regs: Part 21 (certification), Part 43 (maintenance), Part 91 (operating).
  3. AC = advisory (not binding). AD = mandatory (Part 39). Regulation = law; guidance = advice.
  4. FAA Form 337 = Major Repair and Alteration. Original to owner; copy to FAA (Oklahoma City).
  5. Minor = acceptable data + logbook. Major = approved data + Form 337.
  6. 43.9 = maintenance entry; 43.11 = inspection entry (both What / When / Who).
  7. 91.417 TEMPORARY (a)(1)/(b)(1): maint., prev. maint., alterations, all inspections - kept 1 yr / until repeated or superseded.
  8. 91.417 PERMANENT (a)(2)/(b)(2): total time in service, life-limited parts, time since overhaul, current inspection status, current AD status, Form 337s - kept while owned & TRANSFERRED WITH THE AIRCRAFT.
  9. OVERHAUL = service limits; REBUILT = new-part tolerances. Rebuilt engine can get "zero time."
  10. TCDS = spec sheet, part of the TC. STC = approval of a modification. SAIB = info only.
  11. Standard A/W cert = 8100-2; Special A/W cert = 8130-7 (experimental = pink). Must be displayed in aircraft.
  12. SLSA = factory-built; ELSA = kit-built. LSA uses ASTM (not 337) and manufacturer Safety Directives (not ADs).
